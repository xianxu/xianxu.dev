I've wrote about the birthright citizenship case [here](./birthright-citizenship.md) and [here](./birthright-citizenship-v2.md). Today, June 30th of 2026, final ruling's out, a 6-3 on both statutory or constitution ground, or 5-4 on constitutional ground alone. My prediction's right in the middle, the position Kavanaugh took. The ruling is [here](https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/25pdf/25-365_4hdj.pdf). Some reflection here. 

### The Ruling

#### The Holding

> Children born in the United States to parents unlawfully or temporarily present are “subject to the jurisdiction” of the United States and are citizens at birth under the Fourteenth Amendment’s Citizenship Clause.

#### The Vote

ROBERTS, C. J., delivered the opinion of the Court, in which SOTOMAYOR, KAGAN, BARRETT, and JACKSON, JJ., joined. JACKSON, J., filed a concurring opinion, in which SOTOMAYOR, J., joined as to the introduction and Part I. 

The majority further sanctioned the prevailing view of birthright citizenship: you are born here, you are a citizen. No law can be passed to narrow it, sans a constitutional amendment. 

KAVANAUGH, J., filed an opinion concurring in the judgment and dissenting in part. He would have ruled against Trump on statutory ground, but leave the door open for congress to further change the list of exclusion from birthright citizenship. While that's a position I predicted, nonetheless the way he argued about it seems logically inconsistent: 

1. that the exclusion list in 14th amendment is amorphous
2. that Wong Kim Ark's closed-set enumeration is wrong
3. that 14th amendment itself doesn't conflict with Trump's executive order
4. that INA (Immigration and Nationality Act) with exactly same wording to 14th amendment somehow conflicts with Trump's order 

THOMAS, J., filed a dissenting opinion, in which GORSUCH, J., joined. ALITO, J., and GORSUCH, J., filed dissenting opinions. They view domicile as one critical criteria of being citizen. 

### Constitutional vs Statutory Ground

Since Trump used executive order, there are two ways it may be struck down, on finding it contravene with the constitution itself, or merely in conflict with federal laws. In this case, the court found it contravenes with the constitutional itself, 14th amendment in particular. This means a new constitutional precedence is established, together with Wong Kim Ark, strengths the US's practice of granting almost everyone citizenship, when they are born on US soil. 

The court's ruling today, I think, pretty clearly foreclosed future challenges sans a constitutional amendment on this issue. And it's a good thing. 

### Some Facets and Reflection

Both sides called to rhetoric, Roberts referred to Thomas Paine's the new country as "asylum for mankind" while Thomas called upon Frederick Douglass for citizens to "fought and bled in the same battles”, “gained and gloried in the same victories", “liable to be called upon to defend in time of war”. 

The majority relied on common law tradition. They believe the citizenship is the fundamental "right to have rights", and keep it a simple and unambiguous line. I'm an engineer, Occam's razor you know. 

The dissenting called common law's jus solis a feudal relic that American republic should throw away, replace with the domicile requirement. While I'm sympathetic on this argument; who would like to be a feudal subject to a crown and who wouldn't like to create a new shining principled republic? The only problem is history was littered with politicians and lawyers playing word game when you leave holes in this fundamental rights (think of Dred Scott). 

On the eve of 250th birthday of United States, that's founded on the audacious idea of "all men are created equal", a polity conceived to be bound by such idea, not by culture, language, religion, or blood. It's fitting that it keeps its door open, maybe sometimes too widely open. That's the defining character of America: "Give me your tired, your poor, Your huddled masses yearning to breathe free". That's the fundamental strength of United States.
